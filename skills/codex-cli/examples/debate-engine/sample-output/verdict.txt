**Verdict**

AI should not replace teachers overall, though it should replace some teaching tasks. The pro-replacement case is strong on personalization, scalability, access, and cost: AI can deliver adaptive practice, instant feedback, translation, and around-the-clock support in ways human systems often cannot. But the anti-replacement case is stronger because it covers the full job of teaching, not just instruction delivery. Across the practitioner, skeptic, historian, and futurist views, the most persuasive point is that education depends on human judgment, trust, motivation, safeguarding, social development, and accountability, and past technologies have consistently complemented rather than eliminated teachers. On balance, AI is best understood as a powerful co-teacher, not a full substitute.

**Strongest argument from each side**

- `For`: AI can provide highly personalized, always-available instruction at massive scale, which could improve access and learning quality, especially where teacher shortages are severe.
- `Against`: Teaching is not just content delivery; it requires human judgment, relationship-building, classroom management, ethical responsibility, and emotional support that current AI cannot reliably provide.

**Recommended course of action**

Use AI to automate routine instructional work: tutoring, practice, feedback, lesson drafting, translation, and accessibility support. Keep human teachers in the accountable lead role for motivation, classroom culture, student safety, adaptation, and final judgment. The best path is a hybrid model: more AI leverage, not teacher removal.
